Amazon Redesign

Our second project was to take an existing e-commerce brand and apply UX principles to solve user's problems. We did this through exstensive research and testing. This project was made in collaboration with Misha, Merritt, and Darsh


Analyzing Amazon's Existing Flow

Before we conducted surveys and interviews, we first did a SWOT Analysis to determine the strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats to Amazon.

Our biggest take-aways from this were that the existing Amazon experience had some flaws in the filtering options and the navigation menu.

Studying the Navigation

After doing the SWOT analysis, we decided to analyze the way a user would interact with Amazon if they didn't know what they wanted and were using the menu.

Analyzing the navigation flow really highlighted for us just how much of an opportunity the filters were. Many pages had inconsistant filter options between similar products, such as only showing 2 color options for paint, or showing the price filter in a horizontal bar but the other prices on a vertical bar.

Honing in on the Features

Having now completed a SWOT Analysis and Navigation Flow Analysis, we had a clearer idea of how to proceed. We then created a Feature Benefit Matrix to help visualize our hypothesis.

Sending out the Surveys

The next step in the research phase was the surveys. We created a survey with 13 questions and sent it out. We recieved 28 responses which confirmed our hypothesis but also provided some surprising insights.

Interviews

After receiving the survey results, we interviewed 3 people in the average demographic. Below are some of their quotes about their shopping habits and Amazon in general.


  • “Usually the search is enough to get me to where I’m going.”

  • “The site just feels cluttered and busy, I feel trapped when I shop.”

The responses also lined up with the survey results, showing that people mostly use the search bar and also feel that the current Amazon flow is "cluttered."

The Persona Touch

All of the research lead us to a clear vision of our end-user, Kelly Morgan.

What is Kelly Thinking?

After creating Kelly, we wanted to empathize with her even further. To this end, we created an Empathy Map to really get into her mindset.

Kelly's Story - Visualized

The next step was to create a storyboard of how Kelly currently interacts with Amazon, to further empathize with her.

Kelly's Journey

The journey map we made for Kelly helped us be able to keep track of her emotions during the process, as well as opportunities where our design could help her.

Our Problem Statement

We believe that navigating the Amazon website is a challenge for consumers because of information overload, particularly overload of irrelevant information.

Eliminating unnecessary information while focusing on the relevant information may solve this problem so users can avoid information overload during their journey while also helping Amazon establish credibility and trust and strengthen relationships with consumers post-purchase.

We will know we have succeed when 80% of our customers have rated the shopping experience on Amazon as satisfactory and have increased our customer retention rate by 10%.

Beginning the Design Phase

With the user flow charted out, we now had a clear plan of action and created what we thought were the perfect amount of wireframes...

Testing our designs and our assumptions

With the first round of wireframes done, we set out to test over 2 days. To this end, we went to the Austin Public Library on Saturday and Radio Coffee on Sunday, with iteration between.

    From this round of testing we discovered 3 key insights:

  • People Don't Like Pop-Up Modals
    All of the testers except one expressed dislike for a pop-up error modal, they would prefer
    to see that on the page or highlighted with color.

  • They Preferred Sign-In Buttons and Labels
    The Majority of the testers also preferred a button that says "Sign In" rather than an icon
    of a person. They also said that if there were icons, they'd want them to have
    labels underneath so they could easily identify what they represent.

  • They Liked the Filter Options
    All of the testers found the way the filters work was intuitive. Limiting the choices on
    the search page, and having some filters be on the product page itself.

After this round of testing, we utilized the feedback and iterated on our wiresframes.

We quickly iterated and made changes based on the feedback we got from Saturday and tested again on Sunday.

Above are some of the notes we took from Sunday's tests, these provide similar feedback as Saturday, so we knew we were on the right track.

Creating the Style Guide

Now that we had researched, designed, tested, and iterated, we were finally ready to start designing. We set forth and made a style guide to keep our designs consistant across the group.

3 Flows to Rule Them

With the style guide finalized, we started designing the three flows. Sign-In, Purchase, and Error/Payment.

Reflections for the Next Sprint

Having completed the three high-res comps, and presenting it to the class, we were now done with this sprint. Overall, we felt it was a successful solution based on data and user's needs. However, there is always room to improve.


    If we had time for another sprint we would have changed the following:

  • More Rounds of Testing
    Our testing was done in the wireframes stage and going forward we would test
    our high res comps to get even more feedback and improve the flows.

  • Refine the Style Guide a Bit More
    Our style guide succeeded at making the designs look cohesive and went
    into details such as box-shadows being "X: 2, Y: 2, Blur: 8, and Color: rgb(51,51,51)"
    but I would have liked to have created more detailed components
    and perhaps even more shades of colors.

  • A Coded/Developed MVP
    For the deliverables, we only made hi-res comps in jpeg/png form.
    However, I would have liked to have made a working prototpye in
    HTML, CSS, and Javascript.

This project gave us a greater understanding of just how much work goes into e-commerce
websites, there are so many moving parts that you could spend years just testing what already
exists. Going forward, I would love the opportunity to work on the development of an
e-commerce site to see how it comes together on the front-end and back-end.
